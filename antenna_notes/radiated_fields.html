What causes radiation?

Radiation is the result of charges accelerating or decelerating over time. Recall that a charge creates an electric field around it that radiates outwards either forever or until influenced. When a charge moves, the electric field created by that charge also moves. As a result of Ampere’s Law, the changing electric field creates a magnetic field that wraps around the charge.

Because the electric field can only move so fast, the changes in the electric and magnetic fields propagate away from the moving charge. This is what we call radiation.

So: Radiation is caused by the acceleration or deceleration of charges.

Radiated Power Flow Density

The power flow density of an electromagnetic field, Sˉ\bar{S}Sˉ, is given by one-half of the cross product between Eˉ\bar{E}Eˉ and Hˉ∗\bar{H}^*Hˉ∗.

Sˉ=12Eˉ×Hˉ∗ \bar{S} = \frac{1}{2}\bar{E}\times\bar{H}^* Sˉ=21​Eˉ×Hˉ∗

Note that Sˉ\bar{S}Sˉ is:

  1. A vector quantity
  2. Not the amount of power at some location

Sˉ\bar{S}Sˉ represents the amount of power flowing in a particular direction through some area. It is not a measure of power, but rather power density.

Sˉ\bar{S}Sˉ is a complex value. The imaginary component, which represents the energy stored in the form of standing waves, has no time-average value and therefore does not represent power that can be delivered to some object. Only the real component can have a non-zero time-average value.
